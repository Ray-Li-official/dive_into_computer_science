Ray Tracing

Why Ray Tracing​

The use of ray tracing is to make images more realistic, in the following aspects:

  • Shadows
  • Reflections: mirror and glossy
  • Transparent: water, class, etc.
  • Inter-reflection: color bleeding
  • etc.

Note all these operations are difficult in OpenGl pipeline and easy in raytracing framework.

Ray​

Definition​

Definition of Ray

A ray p\mathbf{p}p is defined by a point p0\mathbf{p}_0p0​ and a direction d\mathbf{d}d and is mathematically expressed as following:

p(t)=p0+dt  t>0\mathbf{p}(t) = \mathbf{p}_0 + \mathbf{d}t\ \ t>0p(t)=p0​+dt  t>0

Ray Through Pixel Formula

If we have a camera with position and orientation described as following:

eye∈R3, u∈R3, v∈R3, w∈R3\mathbf{eye}\in\mathbb{R}^3,\ \mathbf{u}\in\mathbb{R}^3,\ \mathbf{v}\in\mathbb{R}^3,\ \mathbf{w}\in\mathbb{R}^3eye∈R3, u∈R3, v∈R3, w∈R3

and a zero-centered screen following paramters:

a=widthheight, fovy,a = \frac{\text{width}}{\text{height}}, \ \textit{fovy},a=heightwidth​, fovy,

where aaa is the aspect ration and fovy\textit{fovy}fovy is the field of view angle.


Then the ray p\mathbf{p}p of the zero-indexed and bottom-left to top-left pixel (i,j)(i,j)(i,j) on the screen, in the world, is given by:

p(t)=p0+dt  t>0p0=eyed=Normalize(α⋅a⋅tan⁡(fovy2)u+β⋅tan⁡(fovy2)v−w)\mathbf{p}(t) = \mathbf{p}_0 + \mathbf{d}t\ \ t>0\\\mathbf{p}_0=\mathbf{eye}\\ \mathbf{d}=\text{Normalize}(\alpha \cdot a \cdot \tan(\frac{\textit{fovy}}{2})\mathbf{u}+\beta\cdot\tan(\frac{\textit{fovy}}{2})\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{w})p(t)=p0​+dt  t>0p0​=eyed=Normalize(α⋅a⋅tan(2fovy​)u+β⋅tan(2fovy​)v−w)

where

α=2⋅i+0.5width−1, β=1−2⋅j+0.5height\alpha = 2 \cdot \frac{i+0.5}{\text{width}}-1, \ \beta = 1-2\cdot \frac{j+0.5}{\text{height}}α=2⋅widthi+0.5​−1, β=1−2⋅heightj+0.5​

Explain​

d\mathbf{d}d is a unit vector representing the direction from eye\mathbf{eye}eye to the center of (i,j)(i,j)(i,j) pixel on the screen, in world coordinate.

STEP 1, write d\mathbf{d}d in term of camera coodinate:

[∣d∣0]=Normalize(C∗[∣dC∣0])\begin{bmatrix} | \\ \mathbf{d} \\ | \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \text{Normalize}(\mathbf{C} * \begin{bmatrix} | \\ \mathbf{d}_C \\ | \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} )⎣⎡​∣d∣0​⎦⎤​=Normalize(C∗⎣⎡​∣dC​∣0​⎦⎤​)

Where dC\mathbf{d}_CdC​ is the direction from from eye\mathbf{eye}eye to the center of (i,j)(i,j)(i,j) pixel on the screen, in camera coordinate and C\mathbf{C}C is the camera matrix, such that:

C=[∣∣∣∣uvweye∣∣∣∣0001]\mathbf{C} = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & | & |\\ \mathbf{u} & \mathbf{v} & \mathbf{w} & \mathbf{eye}\\ | & | & | & |\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}C=⎣⎡​∣u∣0​∣v∣0​∣w∣0​∣eye∣1​⎦⎤​

STEP 2, rewrite dC\mathbf{d}_CdC​ as following:

dC=qC−eyeC\mathbf{d}_C = \mathbf{q}_C - \mathbf{eye}_CdC​=qC​−eyeC​

where qC\mathbf{q}_CqC​ is the position of the center of the (i,j)(i,j)(i,j) pixel and eyeC\mathbf{eye}_CeyeC​ is the position of the eye, both in camera coordinate.

STEP 3, find out qC\mathbf{q}_CqC​ and eyeC\mathbf{eye}_CeyeC​. It's quite obvious we have:

eyeC=(0,0,0)\mathbf{eye}_C = (0,0,0)eyeC​=(0,0,0)

For qC\mathbf{q}_CqC​, we first convert i,ji,ji,j into ratio relationships α,β\alpha, \betaα,β and then scaled by the screen size:

qC=(α⋅a⋅tan⁡(fovy2),β⋅tan⁡(fovy2),−1)α=2⋅i+0.5width−1, β=1−2⋅j+0.5height\mathbf{q}_C = (\alpha \cdot a \cdot \tan(\frac{\textit{fovy}}{2}),\beta\cdot\tan(\frac{\textit{fovy}}{2}),-1)\\ \alpha = 2 \cdot \frac{i+0.5}{\text{width}}-1, \ \beta = 1-2\cdot \frac{j+0.5}{\text{height}}qC​=(α⋅a⋅tan(2fovy​),β⋅tan(2fovy​),−1)α=2⋅widthi+0.5​−1, β=1−2⋅heightj+0.5​

Ray-Geometry Intersection​

Definition​

Definition of Ray-Geometry Intersection

The name itself is already pretty self-explained: it's the intersection between one geometry, like triangle, spheres, etc., and one ray.

Formulas​

Ray-triangle intersection formula

If we are given a ray(p0,d\mathbf{p}_0,\mathbf{d}p0​,d) and a triangle(p1,p2,p3\mathbf{p}_1,\mathbf{p}_2,\mathbf{p}_3p1​,p2​,p3​) and the solutions for the following equation system:

[∣∣∣∣p1p2p3−d∣∣∣∣1110] [λ1λ2λ3t]=[∣p0∣1]\begin{bmatrix} | & | & | & |\\ \mathbf{p}_1 & \mathbf{p}_2 & \mathbf{p_3} & -\mathbf{d}\\ | & | & | & |\\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\ \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1\\ \lambda_2\\ \lambda_3\\ t\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} |\\ \mathbf{p}_0\\ |\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix}⎣⎡​∣p1​∣1​∣p2​∣1​∣p3​∣1​∣−d∣0​⎦⎤​ ⎣⎡​λ1​λ2​λ3​t​⎦⎤​=⎣⎡​∣p0​∣1​⎦⎤​

are all non-negative, i.e., λ1,λ2,λ3,t≥0\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,t \geq 0λ1​,λ2​,λ3​,t≥0, then we have:

q=λ1p1+λ2p2+λ3p3n=λ1n1+λ2n2+λ3n3\mathbf{q} = \lambda_1\mathbf{p}_1 + \lambda_2\mathbf{p}_2+ \lambda_3\mathbf{p}_3\\ \mathbf{n} = \lambda_1\mathbf{n}_1 + \lambda_2\mathbf{n}_2+ \lambda_3\mathbf{n}_3\\q=λ1​p1​+λ2​p2​+λ3​p3​n=λ1​n1​+λ2​n2​+λ3​n3​

where q\mathbf{q}q is the intersection position and n\mathbf{n}n is the surface normal.

Ray-sphere intersection formula

If we are given a ray(p0,d\mathbf{p}_0,\mathbf{d}p0​,d) and a sphere(c,r\mathbf{c},rc,r), then we can construct the following equation system:

(p0+td)2=r2(\mathbf{p}_0+t\mathbf{d})^2 = r^2(p0​+td)2=r2

where the solution formula would be:

t=−d⋅(p0−c)±⋅⋅=(d⋅(p0−c))2−∣p0−c∣2+r2t = - \mathbf{d}\cdot(\mathbf{p}_0 - \mathbf{c}) \pm \sqrt{\cdot}\\ \sqrt{\cdot}= \sqrt{(\mathbf d \cdot (\mathbf{p}_0 - \mathbf{c}))^2-|\mathbf{p}_0-\mathbf{c}|^2 + r^2}t=−d⋅(p0​−c)±⋅​⋅​=(d⋅(p0​−c))2−∣p0​−c∣2+r2​
  • If ⋅\sqrt{\cdot}⋅​ is undefined, then there's no intersection
  • If ⋅=0\sqrt{\cdot} = 0⋅​=0, then there's one intersection(tangent)
  • If ⋅>0\sqrt{\cdot} > 0⋅​>0, then there's two intersections and we pick the smallest positive ttt
Ray-transformed intersection formula

If we are given a ray(p0,d\mathbf{p}_0,\mathbf{d}p0​,d), a transformed object that we know the formula to calculate the intersection before the transformation.

Then we can firstly find out the ray(p0M,dM\mathbf{p}_{0M}, \mathbf{d}_Mp0M​,dM​) in the model view coordinate:

[∣p0M∣1]=M−1[∣p0∣1],  [∣dM∣0]=M−1[∣d∣0]\begin{bmatrix} |\\ \mathbf{p}_{0M}\\ |\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{M}^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} |\\ \mathbf{p}_{0}\\ |\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix},\ \ \begin{bmatrix} |\\ \mathbf{d}_{M}\\ |\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{M}^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} |\\ \mathbf{d}\\ |\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix}⎣⎡​∣p0M​∣1​⎦⎤​=M−1⎣⎡​∣p0​∣1​⎦⎤​,  ⎣⎡​∣dM​∣0​⎦⎤​=M−1⎣⎡​∣d∣0​⎦⎤​

Find out the desired intersection qM\mathbf{q}_MqM​ and then convert it to world coordinate:

[∣q∣1]=M[∣qM∣1],\begin{bmatrix} |\\ \mathbf{q}\\ |\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{M} \begin{bmatrix} |\\ \mathbf{q}_M\\ |\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix},⎣⎡​∣q∣1​⎦⎤​=M⎣⎡​∣qM​∣1​⎦⎤​,

Global Illumination​

Definition​

Definition of Global Illumination

Local illumination means direct lightening and when we add indirect lightening into local illumination, it's called global illumination.

Recursive Model​

There are more than one model used to output the final light color. The following model is one of the most general shading models:

The general shading model

If we are given the folling parameters:

  • v\mathbf{v}v: direction to the source of incoming ray
  • n\mathbf{n}n: surface normal
  • p\mathbf{p}p: position of the intersection
  • Cdiffuse,Cspecular\mathbf{C_{diffuse}},\mathbf{C_{specular}}Cdiffuse​,Cspecular​: Material Colors

then we have the follow model:

Lseen={CdiffuseL(p,d)(n⋅d) with probability 0.5orCspecularL(p,r) with probability 0.5\mathbf{L_{seen}} = \begin{cases} \mathbf{C_{diffuse}}\mathbf{L}_{(\mathbf{p,d})}(\mathbf{n\cdot d})\textit{ with probability 0.5}\\ \text{or}\\ \mathbf{C_{specular}}\mathbf{L}_{(\mathbf{p,r})}\textit{ with probability 0.5}\\ \end{cases}Lseen​=⎩⎨⎧​Cdiffuse​L(p,d)​(n⋅d) with probability 0.5orCspecular​L(p,r)​ with probability 0.5​

where d\mathbf{d}d is a random direction that's unifornly distributed; L(p,d)\mathbf{L}_{(\mathbf{p,d})}L(p,d)​ is the light color seen by ray (p,d)(\mathbf{p,d})(p,d) and L(p,r)\mathbf{L}_{(\mathbf{p,r})}L(p,r)​ is the light color seen by ray (p,r)(\mathbf{p,r})(p,r).

Note a max depth is required for this recursive model. When the max depth is reached, simply set the light color to the traditional diffuse function:

Ldefault=∑i∈lightsmax⁡(n⋅li,0)visibilityiCdiffuseLlight source i\mathbf{L_{default}} = \sum_{i \in \text{lights}}\max(\mathbf{n\cdot l_i,0})\text{visibility}_i\mathbf{C_{diffuse}}\mathbf{L_{\text{light source }i}}Ldefault​=i∈lights∑​max(n⋅li​,0)visibilityi​Cdiffuse​Llight source i​

Recursive Model: Explained​

The genral shading model is straight forwardly iterated from the Direct shading model:

Lseen=∑i∈lightsmax⁡(n⋅li,0)CdiffuseLlight source i+CspecularBlinnphong(v,n,light sources)\begin{align*} \mathbf{L_{seen}} = &\sum_{i \in \text{lights}}\max(\mathbf{n\cdot l_i,0})\mathbf{C_{diffuse}}\mathbf{L_{\text{light source }i}}\\ &+ \mathbf{C_{specular}}\text{Blinnphong}(\mathbf{v,n},\text{light sources}) \end{align*}Lseen​=​i∈lights∑​max(n⋅li​,0)Cdiffuse​Llight source i​+Cspecular​Blinnphong(v,n,light sources)​

Step 1, add shadow:

Lseen=∑i∈lightsmax⁡(n⋅li,0)visibilityiCdiffuseLlight source i+CspecularBlinnphong(v,n,light sources)\begin{align*} \mathbf{L_{seen}} = &\sum_{i \in \text{lights}}\max(\mathbf{n\cdot l_i,0})\text{visibility}_i\mathbf{C_{diffuse}}\mathbf{L_{\text{light source }i}}\\ &+ \mathbf{C_{specular}}\text{Blinnphong}(\mathbf{v,n},\text{light sources}) \end{align*}Lseen​=​i∈lights∑​max(n⋅li​,0)visibilityi​Cdiffuse​Llight source i​+Cspecular​Blinnphong(v,n,light sources)​

Step 2, change to recursive reflection:

Lseen=CdiffuseL(p,d)(n⋅d)+CspecularL(p,r)\begin{align*} \mathbf{L_{seen}} = &\mathbf{C_{diffuse}}\mathbf{L}_{(\mathbf{p,d})}(\mathbf{n\cdot d})\\ &+ \mathbf{C_{specular}}\mathbf{L}_{(\mathbf{p,r})} \end{align*}Lseen​=​Cdiffuse​L(p,d)​(n⋅d)+Cspecular​L(p,r)​​

Step 3, Combine two terms to avoid exponential growth:

Lseen={CdiffuseL(p,d)(n⋅d) with probability 0.5orCspecularL(p,r) with probability 0.5\mathbf{L_{seen}} = \begin{cases} \mathbf{C_{diffuse}}\mathbf{L}_{(\mathbf{p,d})}(\mathbf{n\cdot d})\textit{ with probability 0.5}\\ \text{or}\\ \mathbf{C_{specular}}\mathbf{L}_{(\mathbf{p,r})}\textit{ with probability 0.5}\\ \end{cases}Lseen​=⎩⎨⎧​Cdiffuse​L(p,d)​(n⋅d) with probability 0.5orCspecular​L(p,r)​ with probability 0.5​

Samples and average​

Note the model above won't be accurate at all because d\mathbf{d}d is a random direction. A simple solution is just use multiple samples rather than one:

Averaging model
Lcum=∑NLseen,  Lpixel=LcumN\mathbf{L_{cum}} = \sum^{N}\mathbf{L_{seen}},\ \ \mathbf{L_{pixel}} = \frac{\mathbf{L_{cum}}}{N}Lcum​=∑N​Lseen​,  Lpixel​=NLcum​​

where NNN is the number of samples.

Indefinite max depth​

One might find that max depth is a really important paramter for the model. Max depth is also the number for the ray to bounce between the objects. Instead of choosing the fixed number for this parameter, let the ray bounce indefinitely until randomly terminated:

method of Russian Roulette

Given a termination probability ppp, we have:

pk=(1−p)kp,wk=1(1−p)kpp_k = (1-p)^kp, w_k=\frac{1}{(1-p)^kp}pk​=(1−p)kp,wk​=(1−p)kp1​

where pkp_kpk​ is the probablity to get path with kkkth bounce and wkw_kwk​ is its weight.
